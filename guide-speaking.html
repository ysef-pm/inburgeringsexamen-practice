Am I ready for the inburgering speaking exam?

A practical self-check — updated July 2026

Of the five inburgering exam parts, Spreken is the one people are least sure about. Reading and listening you can score at home; speaking feels impossible to judge on your own. It isn't. Here's how to know where you stand.

What the exam actually expects

The standard route tests speaking at A2 level. That means short, everyday exchanges: introducing yourself, describing your work or family, making an appointment, reacting to a simple situation. You watch short video or picture prompts on a computer and record your answers — there is no live examiner conversation.

Your recordings are scored on five things: adequacy (did you actually answer the question?), vocabulary, grammar, fluency, and pronunciation. Two points each, ten in total. Notice what's not on that list: sounding Dutch, speaking fast, or making zero mistakes. A2 tolerates plenty of errors — as long as a patient listener understands you and you answer the question that was asked.

The five-question self-check

Answer honestly. Can you, right now, out loud, in Dutch:

  1. Introduce yourself in 5–6 sentences — name, country, where you live, what you do, your family — without stopping to think?
  2. Describe a simple picture (a market, a classroom, a birthday) in 3–4 full sentences?
  3. Answer "Wat heb je gisteren gedaan?" with correct past tense on the common verbs (ik ben gegaan, ik heb gewerkt)?
  4. React to an everyday situation: your child is sick, you call the school — what do you say?
  5. Keep talking for 20 seconds on a familiar topic without switching to English?

Score yourself: 4–5 confident yeses → you're likely exam-ready, book a slot and polish. 2–3 → the foundation is there; two focused weeks can close the gap. 0–1 → build daily speaking habits first, the exam can wait a month or two.

The mistakes that actually cost points

  • Not answering the question. Adequacy is graded first. A beautiful sentence about the wrong topic scores zero. Listen to the prompt twice in practice; answer that.
  • One-word answers. "Ja" is not an A2 answer. Aim for two full sentences every time.
  • Long silences. Fluency suffers more from 5-second gaps than from small grammar slips. A filler like "nou, even denken…" is better than silence — and very Dutch.
  • Cramming difficult words. B1 vocabulary used wrongly scores worse than A2 vocabulary used correctly.

How to practise when you have no speaking partner

The exam format is actually good news here: since you're recorded answering prompts, you can replicate the exam alone. Record yourself daily answering one everyday question, listen back once, and redo it. What you're training is not perfection — it's the reflex of producing full Dutch sentences on demand.
