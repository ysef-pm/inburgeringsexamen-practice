## Q1: "Is the inburgeringsexamen hard? I'm scared I'm not ready."

The honest answer: the language parts are A2, which is genuinely reachable — short
everyday sentences, not fluent Dutch. What trips people up isn't level, it's format:
audio plays ONCE in luisteren, speaking is answering recorded prompts with no human
in front of you, and schrijven is graded on a strict rubric where small recurring
errors (capitals, word order) leak points.

Two practical tips: (1) practise in exam format, timed and one-attempt, not with
apps that let you replay everything; (2) figure out your weakest part *before*
booking — you pass per part, so one weak skill means a re-sit for that part only.

## Q2: "How do I practise speaking for the exam without a partner?"

Good news: the spreekexamen has no conversation partner anyway — you record answers
to video prompts. So solo practice actually matches the exam. Daily routine that
works: record yourself answering one everyday question (60s), listen back once,
redo it. You're training the reflex of producing full sentences on demand, not
perfection. Grading is adequacy/vocabulary/grammar/fluency/pronunciation, 2 points
each — and "adequacy" (did you answer the actual question) is where most points die,
not accent.

categories: ratemydutch.com + disclosure.)*

## Q3: "Luisteren is impossible, the audio goes too fast"

The killer is that fragments play once — most people practise with replayable audio
and then panic in the real thing. Train one-time listening specifically: podcast/
jeugdjournaal clip, no pausing, then answer three questions: who spoke, what did
they want, what must happen next. Also drill numbers/times/dates separately; half
of the exam questions hinge on catching one specific fact.

## Q4: "What order should I do the exam parts in? / Which is hardest?"

Book KNM early — it's pure learnable knowledge (health care, work, school,
government) and a quick confidence win. Lezen next for most people. Save spreken/
schrijven for last since productive skills improve slowest. But this varies a lot
per person — diagnose yourself first instead of following anyone's default order.

## Q5: "Failed schrijven twice, what am I doing wrong?"

Almost always: execution and mechanics, not vocabulary. The rubric grades execution
first — if you didn't do the task (wrong format, missing element), other points
collapse. Then grammar/spelling: recurring small errors (missing capitals, verb
position after "omdat", d/t endings) cost the same points every time until fixed.
Fix: write one short practical text daily (ziekmelden, appointment, reply to
invitation), then next day rewrite yesterday's fixing exactly one error class.

## Q6: "Any free resources for inburgering practice?"

Always mention first: the official practice exams at inburgeren.nl are free and are
the single best format reference. Beyond that: jeugdjournaal for listening,
nu.nl for reading, and writing practice with any A2 workbook.
